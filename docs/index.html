1.1 Data All Around Us

[cite_start]From the moment you wake up to the time you go to sleep, nearly every action you take can produce data[cite: 106]. [cite_start]Think about how much information you generate in a single day[cite: 105]. [cite_start]Whether you are checking your phone, walking to class, streaming music, or scrolling through social media, you are leaving a digital trail[cite: 107].

Sensors and Sources

Most of this data is captured automatically by sensors embedded in the devices we use every day. * [cite_start]Smartphones: Your phone’s step counter tracks your physical movement[cite: 108]. * [cite_start]Web Browsers: Your history logs your searches and interests[cite: 108]. * [cite_start]Apps: Services like Spotify or Apple Music save your favorite songs and create playlists based on your listening habits[cite: 108]. * GPS: Tracks your location data to help with navigation or tag your photos.

1.2 Types of Data: Structured vs. Unstructured

As we explore the data generated by these devices, it is helpful to categorize it into two main types:

  1. Structured Data: This is highly organized and formatted data that is easily searchable in relational databases.
    • Examples: Your daily step count (a number), dates on a calendar, or an Excel spreadsheet of expenses.
  2. Unstructured Data: This data has no pre-defined format or organization, making it more difficult to collect, process, and analyze.
    • Examples: The body of a text message, video files, photos, or social media posts.

1.3 Activity 1: Log Your Data

Objective: To become aware of the sheer volume of data generated in a typical day.

Instructions: [cite_start]For this activity, take one full day and write down everything you did yesterday[cite: 107]. 1. [cite_start]List Actions: Include things like checking your phone, walking to class, or using social media[cite: 107]. 2. [cite_start]Identify Data: Beside each action, identify what data is created[cite: 108]. * Example: Walking \(\rightarrow\) Step count data. * [cite_start]Example: Searching Google \(\rightarrow\) Browser history logs[cite: 108]. 3. [cite_start]Locate Storage: Try to be specific about where this data is stored (e.g., on your device, in the cloud, or in an app)[cite: 109].

  • [cite_start]How might your data be incomplete or biased? [cite: 113]

[cite_start]Objective: To analyze your own communication patterns using data[cite: 125].

Instructions: [cite_start]Choose a recent text message conversation and record the following structured data[cite: 125]: * [cite_start]Number of messages sent vs. received[cite: 126]. * [cite_start]Average time between responses[cite: 127]. * [cite_start]The most frequently used words or emojis[cite: 128].

Sentiment Analysis: [cite_start]Next, use a Sentiment Analysis Tool to analyze the tone of your messages[cite: 129]. [cite_start]Copy and paste a short section of text into the tool to see if it identifies the tone as positive, negative, or neutral[cite: 130].

1.6 Activity 4: Pose Detection & Computer Vision

[cite_start]Objective: To understand how machines (AI) interpret physical movement and unstructured data[cite: 138].

Instructions: We will use a computer vision tool (like Teachable Machine) to detect movements. 1. [cite_start]Open the pose-detection demo and allow your camera to detect your body[cite: 138]. 2. [cite_start]Move: Try raising your arms, turning sideways, or stepping out of the frame[cite: 139]. 3. [cite_start]Test Limits: Move faster or slower to see how accurately the computer tracks you[cite: 140]. 4. [cite_start]Experiment: Change lighting or camera angles to see what affects detection quality[cite: 141].

ImportantKey Concept

[cite_start]This activity demonstrates how AI vision systems work and where they struggle—a core concept in machine learning[cite: 142].
